\section{Introduction}

The most basic analysis of the possible energy levels of an atom involves
considering the effect of the Coulomb potential on the atom's
electrons \cite{harris, qmech, griffiths_qm}. Relative to these energy levels,
atoms' energies accrue additional shifts when placed in a weak
magnetic field due to several effects associated with their angular
momenta. Three
important such effects are the coupling between electrons' spins and
their own angular momenta, (spin-orbit, annotated SO), the coupling
between electrons' spins and their nuclei's spins
(hyperfine structure, annotated HS), and the weak-field
Zeeman effect (annotated Z) \cite{harris}. Each
effect can be treated as a
first-order perturbation to the electron's original Coulomb
Hamiltonian (annotated C) \cite{harris, qmech, griffiths_qm}:

\begin{equation}
  \hat H = \hat H_\text{C} + \hat
  H_\text{SO} + \hat H_\text{HS} +   \hat H_\text{Z}
  \label{eq:hamiltonian}
\end{equation}

In a weak magnetic field, the electron's intrinsic spin angular momentum
$\mathbf{S}$ and the electron's
orbital angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ couple to form a total angular
momentum $\mathbf{J}$,
quantized independently of $\mathbf{S}$ and $\mathbf{L}$. When the
effects of hyperfine structure are considered, $\mathbf{J}$ couples with
the nuclear spin vector $\mathbf{I}$--producing a total atomic angular
momentum $\mathbf{F}$ \cite{harris}.

When considering spin-orbit
split states subject to hyperfine splitting, electron
states are expressed in a hyperfine-coupled basis, whose members
are eigenstates of the operators $\mathbf{L}^2, \mathbf{S}^2,
\mathbf{J}^2, \mathbf{I}^2, \mathbf{F}^2, F_z$, all simultaneously
diagonalizable \cite{qmech, harris}. These compatible operators give rise
to the quantum numbers $l, s, j, i, f,$ and $m_f$, respectively, and allow the
states to be labeled as $\ket{n\,\ell\,j\,f\,m_f}$ (omitting $s$ and
$i$, which do not change during transitions) \cite{qmech, harris}.

The perturbative energy shifts added to to the Coulomb Hamiltonian
can each be expressed as a function
of these quantum numbers, and thus of the electrons' states
\cite{qmech, harris}:

\begin{equation}
  \braket{\hat H_\text{SO}}
  = \left\langle
  \frac{\mu_0 e^2}{4\pi m_e^2 r^3}\,
  \hat{\mathbf S}\!\cdot\!\hat{\mathbf L}
  \right\rangle
  = \left\langle
  \frac{\mu_0 e^2}{4\pi m_e^2 r^3}
  \right\rangle
  \frac{\hbar^2}{2}\!\left[
    j(j+1)-\ell(\ell+1)-s(s+1)
  \right]
  \label{eq:spinorbit}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \braket{\hat H_\text{HS}}
  = \left\langle
  A\,\hat{\mathbf I}\!\cdot\!\hat{\mathbf J}
  \right\rangle
  = A\,\frac{\hbar^2}{2}\!\left[
    f(f+1)-i(i+1)-j(j+1)
  \right]
  \label{eq:hyperfine}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
  \braket{\hat H_\text{Z}}
  = \left\langle
  \mu_B\, g_F \,\hat{\mathbf F}\!\cdot\!\mathbf B
  \right\rangle
  = \mu_B\, g_F \, B\,\braket{\hat F_z}
  = \mu_B\, g_F \, B\, \hbar m_f
  \label{eq:zeeman}
\end{equation}

where $A$ is the hyperfine constant, varying from system to system.
Comparing orders of magnitudes, spin-orbit coupling produces the
largest energy shifts---followed by hyperfine structure and the
Zeeman effect \cite{harris}.

The rubidium atoms in this experiment are exposed to a magnetic
field $\mathbf{B}_0$ along an axis $\mathbf{z}$, causing the energy splitting
described in Figure \ref{fig:rb87_splitting} and precession about
$\mathbf{B}_0$ with a Larmor frequency $\omega_0$ \cite{harris}.
The atoms are simultaneously exposed to radiofrequency (RF) pulses
$\mathbf{B}_{RF} = B_1 \cos(\omega_1 t) \hat x$ applied transverse to
$\mathbf{B}_0$. These RF pulses are indistinguishable from a pair of
counter-rotating magnetic fields of equal strength and frequency
$\omega_{RF}$, circularly polarized in the $x$-$y$ plane \cite{griff}:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{B}_{1}(t) &= \frac{B_1}{2}\left[\hat x\cos(\omega_{RF} t)+\hat
  y\sin(\omega_{RF} t)\right] \\
  \mathbf{B}_{1}'(t) &= \frac{B_1}{2}\left[\hat x\cos(\omega_{RF} t)-\hat
  y\sin(\omega_{RF} t)\right] \\
  \mathbf{B}_{RF} &= \mathbf{B}_{1} + \mathbf{B}_{1}' = B_1
  \cos(\omega_{RF} t) \hat x
\end{align}

$\mathbf{B}_0$, an azimuthal angular position $\beta$ of $\mathbf{F}$, and a
magnetic dipole moment $\mathbf{\mu}$ precessing about $\mathbf{F}$.

When
$\omega_{RF} = \omega_0 = \dot \beta$,
$\mathbf{B}_1$ appears
stationary in the precession frame---while $\mathbf{B}_{1}'$ appears to
rotate in the opposite direction.
Both $\mathbf{B}_1$ and $\mathbf{B}_{1}'$
cause additional torques
on $\mathbf{\mu}$ \cite{young, knight, harris}. In $\mathbf{\mu}$'s
frame of reference,
$\mathbf{B}_1$ appears
stationary while $\mathbf{B}_{1}'$ appears to rotate in the opposite
direction with frequency $2\omega_{RF} = 2\omega_0$. Thus, $\mathbf{B}_1$ causes
a steady torque $\tau_1$ on $\mu$ while $\mathbf{B}_{1}'$ causes a
time-dependent
torque $\tau_{1}'$ whose effects net to zero across precession
cycles \cite{knight, young}. The aggregate effect is a steady
precession about $\mathbf{B}_1$ with a Larmor frequency $\omega_1$,
causing $\mathbf{F}$ to
gradually change its precession angle $\alpha$ \cite{harris}. Thus, resonant
RF pulses meeting the
resonance condition $\omega_1 = \omega_0$ can cause
changes in $\mathbf{F}_z$, suggesting that they cause transitions in
the $m_F$ quantum number. Using theoretical results for the Larmor
frequency of precession about $\mathbf{B}_0$ \cite{harris, griff,
young}, the resonance condition
can be expressed using the RF frequency $f_\text{rf}$ as:

\begin{equation}
  \frac{g_F \mu_B}{h} B_0 = f_\text{rf}
  \label{eq:resonance_condition}
\end{equation}

A quantum-mechanical analysis produces the exact magnitudes of
the RF-driven energy changes. When an RF pulse $\mathbf{B}_1(t) = B_1
\cos(\omega_{RF} t) \hat x$ is
applied, the atom's briefly gains a new Hamiltonian contribution due
to the coupling between $\mathbf{\mu}$ and $\mathbf{B}_1$: $\hat H_{RF} =
-\mathbf{\mu} \cdot \left( B_1 \cos(\omega_{RF} t) \hat x \right)$
\cite{qmech}.

As Figure \ref{fig:dipole_torque} illustrates, the atom's magnetic dipole
moment $\mathbf{\mu}$ precesses about $\mathbf{F}$; in expectation, it is
proportional to $\mathbf{F}$: $\braket{\mathbf{\mu}} = -\frac{g_F \mu_B}{\hbar}
\mathbf{F}$ \cite{harris}. Thus, one can make the approximation $\mathbf{\mu}
\approx -\frac{g_F
\mu_B}{\hbar} \mathbf{F}$ and $\hat H_{RF}$ becomes:

% H^rf​=−μ⋅Brf​=ℏgF​μB​​F⋅(B1​cos(ωt)x^).

\begin{equation}
  \hat H_{\text{rf}}
  = -\,{\boldsymbol{\mu}}\cdot \mathbf B_{\text{rf}}
  \approx \frac{g_F \mu_B}{\hbar}\mathbf F \cdot \left(B_1 \cos(\omega
  t)\,\hat x\right)
  = \frac{g_F \mu_B B_1}{\hbar}\cos(\omega t)\, \hat F_x
  \label{eq:hamiltonian_rf}
\end{equation}

where $\hat F_x$ is the projection of the total angular momentum
operator $\hat F$ onto the $x$-axis (equivalently, the axis of the RF
pulse). Introduce the operators $\hat F_\pm$ that raise and lower the
$m_F$ quantum number by 1, respectively \cite{qmech}. These are defined by:
\begin{align}
  \hat F_\pm &= \hat F_x \pm i \hat F_y \quad \left (\text{providing
    } \hat F_x =
  \frac{1}{2} (\hat F_+ + \hat F_-) \right )\\
  \hat F_\pm \ket{F, m_F} &= \hbar \sqrt{F(F+1) - m_F(m_F \pm 1)} \ket{F,
  m_F \pm 1}
  \label{eq:ladder_operators}
\end{align}

Inserting \eqref{eq:ladder_operators} into \eqref{eq:hamiltonian_rf}
yields:

\begin{equation}
  \hat H_{\text{rf}} \ket{F, m_F} \propto (\hat F_+ + \hat F_-) \ket{F,
  m_F} \propto \left( \ket {F, m_F + 1 } + \ket {F,
  m_F - 1 } \right)
\end{equation}

indicating that the RF interaction connects states with relative
$m_F$ values of $\pm 1$ \cite{qmech}, consistent with the classical conclusion
that an RF pulse causes changes in $\mathbf{F}_z$ via
magnetic torque effects.

Another avenue providing control over the rubidium atoms' energy levels is
interaction with circularly polarized photons. During these
interactions, the E1 selection rules from Table
\ref{tab:e1_selection_rules_corrected} apply: namely, $\pi' = -\pi$,
$\Delta \ell = \pm 1$, $\Delta j = 0, \pm 1$, and $\Delta m_f = q$
determined by the light's polarization component $q$ \cite{qmech, harris}.
Note that $q$ is $+1$ for
right-circularly polarized light and $-1$ for left-circularly polarized light
\cite{qmech}. This allows transitions between the
ground $5S_{1/2}$ manifold and the excited $5P_{1/2}$ and $5P_{3/2}$
manifolds. Upon spontaneous emission, the atoms return to the ground
$5S_{1/2}$ manifold, resulting in the same $l$ and $j$ quantum
numbers as before absorption. Abstractly, for an atom starting in the
ground manifold,
a complete absorption-emission cycle can be viewed as a process that changes
$f$, $m_f$, or both.

\[
  \ket{n, l^{(0)}, j^{(0)}, f^{(0)}, m_f^{(0)}}
  \xrightarrow{\text{absorption}}
  \ket{n, l^{(1)}, j^{(1)}, f^{(1)}, m_f^{(1)}}
  \xrightarrow{\text{emission}}
  \ket{n, l^{(0)}, j^{(0)}, f^{(2)}, m_f^{(2)}}
\]
\[
  \Downarrow
\]
\[
  \ket{f, m_f} \xrightarrow{\text{absorption-emission}} \ket{f', m_f'}
\]

Note that other short-lived intermediate states in the excited
manifold can be attained between absorption and emission, not
depicted above for clarity. Also of note is that atoms' stints in the
excited manifold are orders of magnitude more short-lived than atoms
in the ground manifold \cite{harris}, suggesting that these
intermediate states are
negligible.

Spontaneous emissions have no bias toward a particular $\Delta
m_f$ value \cite{qmech, harris}. Thus, if the light triggering an
absorption-emission cycle is
right-circularly polarized, possible values of $\Delta m_f$ across a
complete absorption-emission cycle are $\Delta m_f = \Delta
m_f^\text{absorption} +
\Delta m_f^\text{emission} \in \set{-1 + 1, 0 + 1, 1 + 1} = \set{0,
+1, +2}$, creating a bias toward higher $m_f$ values. Consider states
with $F = 2$ and $m_F = 2$ for $^{87}$Rb and $F = 3$ and $m_F = 3$
for $^{85}$Rb. Figure \ref{fig:rb87_splitting} suggests that
a transition with $\Delta m_f = +1$ is forbidden; no states in the
excited manifold have a $m_F$ value of $4$ in $^{85}$Rb's energy level diagram
and no states in the excited manifold have a $m_F$ value of $3$ in
$^{87}$Rb's energy level diagram \cite{harris}. Thus, the stochastic
process $\mathcal P$ governing the redistribution of atomic states through the
ground manifold during repeated absorption-emission cycles provides no
outward flow from this zero-outflow state $\ket{\eta}$\footnote{Allow
  states to be uniquely indexed as $\ket{i}$ for
  brevity, with $i = \eta$ identifying the zero-outflow
state.}---while providing
healthy inward flow. No other states have this property, and $\ket{\eta}$
is reachable from any other state in the ground manifold. Thus,
$\ket{\eta}$ becomes an attractor for the system, with an
attraction basin that is the entire $5S_{1/2}$ ground manifold \cite{strog}.

Thus, from a uniform distribution $\pi_0$ of states in the ground manifold,
the distribution $\pi_n$ of states after $n$ absorption-emission
cycles converges to a delta distribution centered on $\ket{\eta}$ as
$n$ approaches infinity:
\begin{equation}
  \lim_{n \to \infty} \pi_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} (\mathcal P^n[\pi_0]) =
  \delta_{\eta}
\end{equation}

The distribution $\delta_\eta$ is characterized by zero photon
absorption probability, causing the rubidium isotope to entirely transmit
light of the wavelength of the $5S_{1/2} \rightarrow 5P_{1/2}$
transition when in this "pumped" state \cite{harris}.

$\mathcal P$ can be changed by switching on an RF field $\mathbf{B}_1$.
Established above, the RF field drives $\Delta m_f = \pm
1$ transitions through coupling with $\mathbf{\mu}$, removing the
bias toward higher
$m_f$ values and providing outward flow from $\ket{\eta}$. This
produces a new steady-state distribution $\pi_\text{depumped}$,
characterized by a nonzero absorption probability for the $5S_{1/2} \rightarrow
5P_{1/2}$ transition photons and a smaller transmission intensity.

This experiment uses Helmholtz coils to produce magnetic fields. For
a Helmholtz coil pair, the magnetic field at the midpoint is
\begin{equation}
  B = \left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^{3/2}\frac{\mu_0 N I}{R},
  \label{eq:helmholtz_field}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_0$ is the vacuum permeability, $N$ is the turns per coil,
$I$ is the coil current, and $R$ is the coil radius \cite{helm}.

\section{Goal}

This experiment aims to measure the hyperfine Land\'{e}
$g$-factors $g_F$ of the $5S_{1/2}$ ground states of $^{85}$Rb and
$^{87}$Rb. By optically pumping rubidium atoms into a single
Zeeman sublevel and observing RF-induced depumping as a function of
applied magnetic field strength, the resonance condition
\eqref{eq:resonance_condition} is used to extract $g_F$ for each
isotope. Additionally, the characteristic optical pumping time
$\tau$ is measured from the transmission signal recovery, and the
cancellation-coil settings are used to recover the local
components of Earth's magnetic field as a consistency check.

\section{Procedure}

The optical rail configuration for optical pumping is shown in Figure
\ref{fig:optical_pumping_setup}, while Figure
\ref{fig:helmholtz_coils} focuses on a Helmholtz coil configuration
used to cancel magnetic fields
in a rubidium absorption cell positioned on the optical rail.
Light from a rubidium discharge lamp
passes through an interference filter to select the appropriate
wavelength for the rubidium $5P_{1/2} \rightarrow
5S_{1/2}$ transition. The light then passes through a
linear polarizer and a quarter-wave plate to produce circularly
polarized light, which is directed through the rubidium absorption
cell. Convergent lenses preseve the intensity of light rays passing
through the apparatus, focusing them onto the rubidium cell and onto
the optical detector. The rubidium cell is placed in a set of three orthogonal
Helmholtz coils, providing a horizontal magnetic field
$\mathbf{B}_0$ along the $z$-axis, a transverse RF magnetic field
$\mathbf{B}_1$
along the $x$-axis, and a vertical field used to cancel Earth's
vertical magnetic field. The optical axis points North to South,
causing Earth's magnetic field and the experimental setup to be coplanar.
Note that in this apparatus orientation, the three coils are capable of
exactly cancelling Earth's magnetic field at the
rubidium absorption cell, allowing for fine control over the magnetic
field used to induce the split states necessary for pumping. The
transmitted light intensity is measured
by an optical detector, providing a measure of how close the system's
atomic state distribution is to $\delta_\eta$.

The horizontal $\mathbf{B}_0$ coils are configured to sweep through a range of
$\mathbf{B}_0$ strengths and thus through Larmor frequencies
$\omega_0$ of the rubidium atoms' angular momenta $\mathbf{F}$. The
RF field frequency $f = \omega_1 / 2\pi$ is
varied to achieve the resonance condition $\omega_1 = \omega_0$
necessary to cause $\Delta m_f = \pm 1$ transitions. This results in
a photodetector signal, proportional to the transmitted light intensity,
that is globally constant with the exception of narrow peaks at which
the light intensity decreases---which indicate satisfaction of the
resonance condition and the loss of the bias toward
$\ket {\eta}$. An example of such a signal is shown in Figure
\ref{fig:resonance_dips}.

The sweep-field strength associated with resonance varies with the predetermined
frequency $\omega_1$ of the RF field. Data points investigating this
relationship are collected, comprising an array of RF frequencies and
an associated array of sweep-field strengths at which resonance is achieved,
organized in Table \ref{tab:gfactor_data} for both $^{85}$Rb and
$^{87}$Rb. Fitting the $f$ vs. resonant $B_0$ relationship expressed
in \eqref{eq:resonance_condition} to these
data points produces
the plots in Figure \ref{fig:gfactor_fits} for both $^{85}$Rb and
$^{87}$Rb and a $g_F$ value derived from the slopes of the fits. Comparisons
between experimental and theoretical $g_F$ values are made in Table
\ref{tab:gfactor_comparison}.

An analysis of the characteristic recovery time of the photodetector
transmission signal is also conducted by fitting an exponential recovery curve
to the data points. The characteristic recovery time is extracted
from the fitted parameters of an exponential recovery curve
and reported in Table \ref{tab:pumping_times} for either isotope.

\section{Discussion}

Pumping of atomic states into the attractor state $\ket \eta$ for both
rubidium isotopes and depumping via an RF field interaction is
successful, causing visible resonance dips in the transmission
intensity of the photons used to bias state transitions when the
sweep field $\mathbf{B}_0$ approaches a value near the resonance
condition. As the applied RF frequency is increased, the resonance
condition is satisfied at a higher
different sweep field strength, as predicted by
\eqref{eq:resonance_condition}. The resonance
dip for rubidium 87 occurs at a lower field strength than that for rubidium 85,
in agreement with \eqref{eq:resonance_condition}:
rubidium 87, with the larger
$g$-factor associated with the relevant hyperfine-split state, requires a
lower field strength to achieve the resonance condition at a fixed RF
frequency. An additional initial dip in transmission intensity is also observed,
confirming that the experimental setup is able to cancel Earth's magnetic field.

Experimental values of $g_F$ for both rubidium isotopes are recovered
to approximately $1\sigma$ as compared with accepted values. The
characteristic time of transmission signal recovery after depumping,
associated with the time required for atomic states to
converge to $\ket{\eta}$, is found to be $11.4 \pm 0.1$ ms for $^{87}$Rb and
$15.4 \pm 0.1$ ms for $^{85}$Rb, agreeing with the expected range of
10--20 ms. Values for Earth's magnetic field are recovered to moderate agreement
with accepted values, with deviations around $1\sigma$.

Likely causes for deviations from accepted values include the
imperfect cancellation of Earth's magnetic field due to misalignment
of the optical rail, inconsistencies in the construction of the
Helmholtz coils, and imperfections in the location of the rubidium
absorption cell within the coils that make the formula
\eqref{eq:helmholtz_field} for the magnetic field at the sample location
slightly invalid.
