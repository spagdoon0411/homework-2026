\subsection*{Marginal Oscillator}

The sample holder contains a marginal oscillator, which is a standard
inductor-capacitor (LC) circuit; The coil of the marginal oscillator
was visible when peering inside of the sample holder hole.
LC circuits have a resonant angular frequency that is a function of their
inductance $L$ and their capacitance $C$:

$$
\omega_\text{LC} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}
$$

where $\omega_\text{LC}$ is the resonant angular frequency of the LC circuit.

The RF frequency knob of the RF signal programmer sets the value of
$C$ such that $\omega_{LC}$ matches the angular frequency of the RF
signal.

\subsection*{Comparing CuSO$_4$-H$_2$O and PTFE}

Unlike the
rest of the samples, whose resonant nucleus is a ${}^1H$ nucleus,
PTFE's (or polytetrafluoroethylene's) resonant nucleus is a
${}^{19}F$ nucleus. This results in
resonant nucleus is a ${}^1H$ nucleus, PTFE's resonant nucleus is a
${}^{19}F$ nucleus. This results in different $g$-factors for the two
samples, $g_H$ (or $g_p$) and $g_F$.

The nuclear $g$-factor can be related to the nuclear spin quantum
number $I$, the magnetic moment $\mu$ of a nucleus in question, and
the nuclear magneton $\mu_N$ by the following equation:

$$
g = \frac{\mu}{\mu_N I}
$$

For ${}^1H$, $I = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\mu_H = 2.782 ... \mu_N$. For
${}^{19}F$, $I = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\mu_F = 2.627... \mu_N$. Both the
${}^1H$ and ${}^{19}F$ nuclei have the same nuclear spin quantum number
$I = \frac{1}{2}$. Additionally, $\mu_H$ and $\mu_F$ are similar,
resulting in a similar (but not identical) $g$-factor for the nuclei.

Although related, the shape of the resonance structure for either
compound does not directly determine the $g$-factor of the
nucleus---which is a function of $\mu$ and $I$.

PTFE, or polytetrafluoroethylene, is a solid and a polymer, whereas
CuSO$_4$-H$_2$O is an ionic compound dissolved in water. PTFE's
nuclei experience deeper potential wells due to
the bonds its atoms participate in. This is expected from a polymer,
especially in comparison to a liquid solution in which the nuclei are
not as constrained.

Immediately after an on-resonance period, this results
in larger dissipation of energy from the PTFE nuclei as compared to
the liquid solutions---and thus an overdamped (decaying exponential)
response instead of an underdamped (oscillatory) response. This
difference is visible between the oscilloscope traces for PTFE and
CuSO$_4$-H$_2$O.

\subsection*{Equal Spacing of Resonance Windows}

The magnetic field causing nuclear precession is sinusoidally
modulated: $\vec B(t) = \vec B_0 + \vec \tilde B
\cos(\omega_\text{s} t)$. A fixed RF frequency $\omega_\text{rf}$
defines a single resonant magnetic field strength $B_\text{resonant}$
at which the resonance
condition $\omega_\text{rf} = \omega_0$ is met, where $\omega_0$ is
the Larmor frequency of precession about $\vec B(t)$.
$B_\text{resonant}$ a constant; the resonance condition must be met
at level sets of $B(t)$ corresponding to $B(t) = B_\text{resonant}$.

Level sets of sinusoids are equally spaced at the equilbrium level,
corresponding to nodes of the sinusoid.

At these nodes, the
approximation $\tilde B \cos(\omega_\text{s} t) \approx \tilde B
\omega_\text{s} t$ is valid and the relationship between $B$ and $t$
is affine and the equilibrium magnetic field level equals the
resonant magnetic field level.

$$
B(t) = B_\text{resonant} + \tilde B \omega_\text{s} t
$$

When the resonance condition is met, the relationship between $B$ and
$\omega_\text{rf}$ is also affine.

$$
\quad \quad
\hbar \omega_\text{rf} = g \mu_N B
$$

Thus, when both conditions are met, the relationship between
$\omega_\text{rf}$ and $t$ is affine. Frequency differences
are proportional to time differences in this regime around sinudoid
nodes and on resonance.

$$
\frac{\hbar \omega_\text{rf}}{g \mu_N} = B_\text{resonant}+ \tilde B
\omega_\text{s} t \Rightarrow d\omega_\text{rf} =
\frac{\omega_\text{s} g \mu_N \tilde B}{\hbar} dt
$$

This allows us to convert the width of a resonance window as measured
on the time axis of the oscilloscope trace of a resonance signal into a
frequency uncertainty using a single factor of proportionality.

\subsection*{Analogies with PHYS 340's Magnetic Torque Lab}

The mangetic torque lab performed in PHYS 340 involved a macroscopic
magnetic dipole precessing about a vertical magnetic field with a
Larmor frequency $\omega_0$. This is analogous to the ensemble-average net
magnetization vector $\vec M$ precessing about a constant-direction
magnetic field $\vec B(t)$ with a Larmor frequency $\omega_0$ in this
NMR experiment.

In the PHYS 340 lab, a pair of magnets was rotated within the dipole's
precession frame, causing a torque on the dipole and inducing a
secondary orthogonal precession about the field $\vec B_1$ caused by
the magnets. This is exactly analogous to an RF pulse lifting nuclear
magnetic dipoles to higher-energy states (by causing $\Delta m_I$ =
$-1$ transitions) through the creation of a circularly polarized
magnetic field $\vec B_1$. Also note that the macroscopic dipole did
not experience the secondary
precession unless the magnets were kept exactly fixed relative to the
dipole; this is analogous to the resonance condition $\omega_1 =
\omega_0$ in this experiment.

\subsection*{Comparison of T2* Values}

All experimental characteristic relaxation times fall within the
range of 0.2 to 1.0 ms. Note that a determination of $T_2^*$ is subject to
variability across different trials of this experiment due to the influence of
the magnetic field inhomogeneity present in the sample holder,
which is highly dependent on our particular apparatus. For reference,
$T_2^*$ is related to the
effective field inhomogeneity $\Delta B$ by:
$$
T_2^* = \frac{1}{\gamma \Delta B} + \gamma \Delta B
$$

where $\gamma$ is the gyromagnetic ratio and $T_2$ is a time constant
associated with spin-spin interaction effects in isolation from field
inhomogeneity effects.

Also note that despite the indistinguisabiliy of the resonant nuclei
across all of the ${}^1H$-nuclei samples (all compounds save for
PTFE), $T_2^*$ values differ significantly due to neighboring atoms
and impurities in the samples.
